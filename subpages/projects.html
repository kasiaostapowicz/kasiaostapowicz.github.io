Research Projects

As a Principal Investigator or a key contributing researcher, dr. Katarzyna Ostapowicz has been actively engaged in a wide range of research initiatives aimed at understanding and monitoring environmental change, with a particular focus on land use and land cover dynamics within the framework of land system science. Her work integrates remote sensing, geospatial analysis, and advanced methods such as artificial intelligence and machine learning to investigate the trajectories, drivers, and impacts of these changes across various spatial and temporal scales.

A core aspect of her research involves the analysis of long-term environmental change, spanning the past 150 years, while also developing projections of future land cover scenarios. These efforts are grounded in specific geographic contexts, including mountainous regions and fragile polar ecosystems. By utilizing both passive and active remote sensing technologies, her research addresses critical challenges such as vegetation health, phenology, and biodiversity loss, providing valuable insights into human-environment interactions and their consequences for sustainability.

Below are listed selected research projects in which she has been a Principal Investigator or an active contributor.

Principal Investigator (PI)

Sentinel-2 Seasonal Composites for Vegetation and Land Cover Monitoring
Research Council of Norway (RCN), 2025, NINA basic funding no.160022/F40/25-25 (NINA, Norway)

The Sentinel-2 Seasonal Composites project aims to enhance vegetation and land cover monitoring in Norway by integrating high-resolution Sentinel-2 data with the long-term Landsat time series. This innovative approach seeks to improve differentiation of low biomass vegetation, such as lichen-dominated habitats, while ensuring temporal consistency and supporting broader ecological applications. Seasonal composites are being developed to capture key vegetative growth periods, offering improved accuracy for vegetation indices like NDVI and GRVI, which are critical for phenological monitoring and understanding carbon cycles. By leveraging advanced processing techniques and validating outputs with field data, the project is expected to provide actionable insights into vegetation dynamics, biodiversity assessments, and land cover changes. The integration of these datasets will safeguard continuity in long-term monitoring as MODIS nears the end of its operational life, supporting sustainable land management and ecosystem studies. The outcomes aim to establish a robust database and advance methodologies, setting a foundation for future research and ecological applications.

POLARSIF Project: Assessing Vegetation Phenological Changes in Polar Regions Using Solar Induced Fluorescence Data
Research Council of Norway (RCN), 2024, NINA basic funding no.160022/F40/24-17 (NINA, Norway)

The POLARSIF project examined vegetation phenological changes in Arctic regions, focusing on Svalbard from 2019 to 2023, using Solar Induced Fluorescence (SIF) data. By integrating ground-based measurements from the FloX spectrometer with satellite data from OCO-2 and Sentinel-5P, the project provided a comprehensive analysis of vegetation dynamics in response to rapid climate changes. POLARSIF leveraged advanced remote sensing techniques to assess greening and browning trends, correlating them with climatic variables such as temperature variations, snowmelt timing, and precipitation changes. This pioneering approach offered direct insights into photosynthetic activity, surpassing traditional indices like NDVI in accuracy and sensitivity, especially in polar environments. The projectâ€™s findings advanced understanding of Arctic vegetation resilience and adaptation under climate stress, setting new standards for monitoring and managing Arctic ecosystems.

TRACE project: Trajectories, causes and consequences of land cover and land use changes in Central Europe
National Science Centre Poland (NCN), Global Land Programme contributing project, 2019-2023, OPUS call, no.2018/29/B/ST10/02979 (Jagiellonian University, Poland)

The TRACE project investigated the trajectories, drivers, and consequences of land cover and land use changes in Central Europe over the past 60 years. By integrating advanced remote sensing data from sources such as Landsat, Sentinel, and Corona missions, the project created detailed spatial databases capturing changes in land use patterns across Czechia, Hungary, Poland, and Slovakia. It focused on key processes, including agricultural land abandonment and peri-urban development, using ensemble modelling and telecoupling frameworks to analyse socio-economic, political, and environmental drivers. The project also assessed the impacts of land cover changes on biodiversity and carbon pools, employing innovative remote sensing methodologies to identify biodiversity indicators and evaluate carbon fluxes. The outcomes included comprehensive land cover maps, improved methods for modelling land use changes, and insights into the interplay between land dynamics and ecosystem services. TRACE contributed to advancing land system science by integrating sustainable development principles, aligning its findings with the UN Sustainable Development Goals, particularly those addressing climate action, life on land, and sustainable communities.

SOCPIX project: Socialising the pixel: detecting and a better understanding of changing land systems with remote sensing and social sciences
Polish National Agency for Academic Exchange (NAWA), 2019-2020, no.PPN/BEK/2018/1/00310 (University of California, Berkeley, USA)

The SOCPIX project integrated remote sensing with social science methods to understand land system changes and their drivers. Combining Earth Observation (EO) data, such as long-term Landsat and Sentinel time series, with socio-ecological datasets, it "socialized the pixel," linking satellite imagery to social, economic, and institutional processes. Key objectives included mapping land use intensification, displacement, and transitions across the Carpathians and Rocky Mountains. The project used the telecoupling framework to explore interactions between distant social-ecological systems, providing insights into land use dynamics. SOCPIX advanced Land System Science by developing integrated frameworks and methodologies, contributing to better land management strategies and understanding of human-environment interactions.

RS4FOR project: Forest change detection and monitoring using passive and active remote sensing data
National Science Centre Poland (NCN), 2016-2019, OPUS call, No.2015/19/B/ST10/02127 (Jagiellonian University, Poland)

The RS4FOR project developed innovative approaches for monitoring forest cover and its dynamics using integrated remote sensing data. Combining optical data from Landsat and Sentinel-2 missions with radar data from Sentinel-1 and LiDAR, the project provided a comprehensive framework for assessing forest cover changes, structure, and health. Focusing on temperate mountain forests in the Polish Carpathians, the project addressed key aspects such as forest cover conversion, biomass estimation, and forest health monitoring. Advanced methods were designed to integrate and process these diverse datasets, enabling precise spatial and temporal analysis. RS4FOR demonstrated the potential of free, publicly available satellite data combined with LiDAR for near-real-time monitoring of forest ecosystems. The project outcomes included algorithms, workflows, and metrics, significantly advancing forest monitoring capabilities and contributing to better management and conservation practices.

LIM project: Integration of categorical- and gradient-based approaches in landscape fragmentation and connectivity modelling using GIS&T
National Science Centre Poland (NCN), SONATA call, 2012-2015, No.2011/03/D/ST10/05568 (Jagiellonian University, Poland)

The LIM project developed advanced methodologies for analysing landscape connectivity and fragmentation by integrating discrete and gradient approaches within unified Landscape Integrated Models. The research focused on the impact of matrix representations (binary, categorical, and continuous) on connectivity assessments, combining habitat suitability and resistance surface analyses to identify critical movement corridors and barriers. These innovative methods were applied in the Carpathian Mountains to study large, protected mammals, including brown bears (Ursus arctos) and European bison (Bison bonasus), whose survival depends on well-connected habitats. The findings are vital for understanding and maintaining ecological networks, offering tools to improve conservation strategies in fragmented landscapes. While directly addressing the needs of Europeâ€™s biodiversity hotspots, the projectâ€™s methodologies and insights have broader global applications, supporting habitat management and conservation planning for large mammals and other species reliant on functional connectivity.

Land use change in period of political transformation and their relationship with natural and socio-economic conditions in the western part of the Beskidy Mountains
Ministry of Science and Education (Poland), PhD project, 2004-2007, No.2P04G07127 (Jagiellonian University, Poland)

The project focused on modelling land use and cover changes in the Western Beskidy Mountains during Poland's political transformation, exploring their relationship with natural and socio-economic factors. Using Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and satellite data, including Landsat TM imagery from 1985 and 2000, the research analysed the spatial and temporal dynamics of land use mosaics. Key outcomes included the development of models to quantify and predict changes, highlighting significant transitions such as agricultural land abandonment and reforestation. The project emphasized the role of environmental variables (elevation, slope) and socio-economic factors (population density, employment structure) in shaping land use patterns. This research provided novel insights into the interactions between policy-driven socio-economic changes and environmental processes in mountainous regions. The findings have implications for sustainable land management, offering tools to understand and manage land use transitions in similar regions globally.
